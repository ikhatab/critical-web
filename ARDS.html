Definition

Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS) is a form of non-cardiogenic respiratory failure characterized by acute onset hypoxemia, bilateral pulmonary infiltrates on imaging, and reduced lung compliance, not fully explained by cardiac failure or fluid overload.

Berlin Definition (2012) Diagnostic Criteria:

Timing

Within 1 week of known clinical insult or new/worsening respiratory symptoms

Chest Imaging

Bilateral opacities not fully explained by effusions, lobar/lung collapse, or nodules

Origin of Edema

Respiratory failure not fully explained by cardiac failure or fluid overload

Oxygenation Severity (with PEEP/CPAP ≥5 cmH₂O):

Severity PaO₂/FiO₂ Ratio Mortality Rate
Mild 200-300 mmHg 27%
Moderate 100-200 mmHg 32%
Severe ≤100 mmHg 45%

Historical Evolution

1967: First Description

Ashbaugh et al. first described a syndrome in 12 patients featuring acute dyspnea, hypoxemia refractory to oxygen, decreased lung compliance, and diffuse bilateral infiltrates.

1994: American-European Consensus Conference (AECC)

Established the first formal criteria, introducing the term Acute Lung Injury (ALI) for PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤ 300 mmHg and ARDS for PaO₂/FiO₂ ≤ 200 mmHg.

Limitations: Poor reliability of chest X-ray interpretation, lack of PEEP standardization, and problematic use of pulmonary artery catheters.

2012: The Berlin Definition

A pivotal evolution, addressing AECC's flaws through empirical evidence.

Key Advances: Defined onset within 1 week of a known insult; mandated minimum PEEP of 5 cm H₂O; replaced PCWP with non-invasive assessment; introduced 3-tier severity stratification with strong prognostic correlation.

Validation: The Berlin Definition showed better predictive validity for mortality compared to the AECC criteria (JAMA 2012;307(23):2526-2533).

Future Directions

The quest for a "biological phenotype" is ongoing. Research into biomarkers (e.g., IL-6, IL-8, sRAGE) aims to move beyond clinical criteria to identify endotypes (e.g., "hyperinflammatory" vs. "hypoinflammatory") for targeted therapies.

Epidemiology

Incidence

Large-scale studies estimate a global incidence of 10-86 cases per 100,000 person-years, with significant regional variation. The COVID-19 pandemic led to an unprecedented surge in ARDS cases worldwide.

Mortality

Mortality is strongly linked to severity, comorbidities, and age. Pre-COVID meta-analyses place overall hospital mortality at approximately 35-45%.

Risk Factors

The most common direct risk factor is pneumonia (bacterial > viral). The most common indirect risk factor is non-pulmonary sepsis. Others include aspiration, trauma, pancreatitis, and massive transfusion.

Pathophysiology

ARDS is characterized by alveolar-capillary barrier injury leading to protein-rich pulmonary edema, reduced lung compliance, ventilation-perfusion mismatch, and severe hypoxemia.

Pathophysiological Phases:

Exudative Phase (0-7 days)

Alveolar edema, inflammatory cell infiltration, hyaline membrane formation, and loss of surfactant function

Proliferative Phase (7-21 days)

Resolution of edema, fibroblast proliferation, and early lung repair

Fibrotic Phase (>21 days)

Collagen deposition and fibrosis in some patients, leading to long-term functional impairment

Key Pathophysiological Concepts:

Diffuse Alveolar Damage (DAD)

The histopathological hallmark, though not universal. Features endothelial and epithelial injury, hyaline membrane formation, and alveolar edema.

"Baby Lung" Concept (Gattinoni)

The aerated lung in ARDS is not a small, healthy lung but a small, normally aerated compartment within a diseased, non-aerated one.

Ventilator-Induced Lung Injury (VILI)

A self-perpetuating injury from mechanical ventilation via volutrauma, barotrauma, atelectrauma, and biotrauma.

Patient-Self Inflicted Lung Injury (P-SILI)

Strong spontaneous inspiratory efforts can generate excessive negative pleural pressures, causing lung injury even without a ventilator.

Clinical Management

General Principles

  • Treat underlying cause (sepsis, pneumonia, trauma, etc.)
  • Supportive care with lung-protective strategies
  • Avoid iatrogenic harm (barotrauma, volutrauma, fluid overload)
  • Multidisciplinary approach with early involvement of intensivists

Mechanical Ventilation: The Cornerstone

The goal is to ensure adequate gas exchange while minimizing VILI.

Lung-Protective Ventilation

  • Tidal volume: 6 mL/kg predicted body weight
  • Plateau pressure: ≤30 cmH₂O
  • Driving pressure: <15 cmH₂O
  • Permissive hypercapnia (pH ≥ 7.15-7.25)

PEEP Strategies

  • Use moderate-to-high PEEP in moderate-severe ARDS
  • Balance recruitment vs overdistension
  • Consider PEEP/FiO₂ tables or esophageal manometry

Adjunctive Strategies

  • Prone positioning for severe ARDS (PaO₂/FiO₂ <150 mmHg)
  • Short-course neuromuscular blockade (≤48h) in early severe ARDS
  • Conservative fluid management

Therapy Indication Considerations
ECMO Severe ARDS with PaO₂/FiO₂ <80 despite optimal care Resource-intensive, requires expertise
Inhaled Pulmonary Vasodilators Refractory hypoxemia as bridge to recovery Transient effect, may improve oxygenation
Recruitment Maneuvers Severe hypoxemia with recruitable lung Potential for hemodynamic compromise

Pharmacotherapy

Corticosteroids

The most evidence-supported pharmacologic adjunct. The DEXA-ARDS trial and subsequent meta-analysis showed that low-dose methylprednisolone reduces mortality and duration of mechanical ventilation in moderate-to-severe ARDS.

Not Recommended

Routine use of β-2 agonists, prostaglandins, surfactant, or statins.
